## Research

### Key findings

- **Adam Grant's Give and Take (2013)** — givers occupy both the bottom AND top of success metrics. The difference: "otherish" givers are generous but protect their time. Selfless givers burn out. In a call center study, givers reminded of their impact raised 171% more money
- **Cialdini's reciprocity principle** — one of the most reliable drivers of human behavior. Small, unexpected gifts significantly increase compliance with later requests. Cross-cultural and deeply ingrained
- **Gary Vaynerchuk's Jab, Jab, Jab, Right Hook** — give value three times before making an ask. Most brands ask too much, too soon
- **The Protege Effect (Chase et al., 2009)** — students who taught material scored significantly higher than those who only studied. Teaching forces deeper processing. Feynman: if you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it
- **Consultative selling outperforms transactional** — Challenger Sale research: top salespeople teach customers something new, leading with insight not product. Gartner: buyers who received educational content were 2.8x more likely to buy with less regret
- **Open source as give-first at scale** — Linux, WordPress (40%+ of the web), Red Hat ($34B acquisition). Give the software away, profit from services and ecosystem
- **Swyx's "Learn in Public"** — share everything you learn as you learn it. Fastest way to build a network and career in tech
- **Bob Burg's Go-Giver** — "Your true worth is determined by how much more you give in value than you take in payment"
- **Zig Ziglar** — "You can have everything in life you want, if you will just help other people get what they want"
- **Generous strategies compound in networks (Gross et al., 2017, PNAS)** — in repeated interactions, generous strategies outperform selfish ones because they build cooperative networks

### Counterarguments

- **Givers at the bottom too** — Grant's paradox. Giving without boundaries leads to burnout and exploitation. The key is being "otherish," not selfless
- **Takers exploit givers** — in opaque, competitive environments, unprotected givers get systematically used
- **Performative generosity gets detected** — when "give first" becomes a known marketing tactic, audiences sense the funnel. The webinar-to-upsell pipeline has trained people to be suspicious
- **Survivorship bias** — we hear about the content creators who gave and succeeded. We don't hear about thousands who gave and got nothing
- **The exposure economy** — "we'll pay you in exposure" weaponizes give-first to justify not paying people. The advice to give freely is easier when you have a safety net
- **Class dimension** — giving first is a luxury. Not everyone can afford to work for free while building reputation

## Suggested Angle

Chris's take: this isn't a strategy, it's a default. You teach, you share, you help — not because you're playing a long game, but because you can't be a taker. The scoring happens or it doesn't.

The interesting tension: in 2026, AI can explain anything. So when a human chooses to explain something to you, it's no longer about the information — it's about the care. The act of explaining becomes a signal of investment. The knowledge is free everywhere; the person choosing to share it with you specifically is what matters.

The counterarguments are real — givers burn out, takers exploit, and "give first" can be a mask for a sales funnel. The honest version isn't performative generosity. It's just... being useful. And trusting that it comes back around.

5. **The AI shift** — When AI can explain anything, what's the value of a human explaining? It's not the knowledge — it's the relationship. Choosing to help someone is a signal AI can't replicate
6. **When it doesn't work** — Givers at the bottom. Takers exploiting generosity. Performative giving that's really just a sales funnel. The exposure economy. Be honest about the limits
7. **The real version** — It's not "give to get." It's "give because you can't not." The scoring is secondary. If you're keeping track, you've already shifted from giving to trading
8. **Close** — You can't be a taker. Not as a moral judgment — as a practical observation. Takers run out of people to take from. Givers build something that compounds
