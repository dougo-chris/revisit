## Research

### Key findings

- **The quote is misattributed** — almost certainly NOT Mark Twain. Likely traces to Josh Billings (Henry Wheeler Shaw), 1874: "It is better to know nothing than to know what ain't so." The modern phrasing is a 20th-century evolution. Al Gore's *An Inconvenient Truth* (2006) boosted it massively, attributed to Twain. No primary source exists. The irony is perfect — a quote about false certainty that is itself falsely attributed with total certainty
- **Overconfidence bias is one of the most robust findings in psychology** — when people say they're "99% certain," they're wrong 20-40% of the time (Lichtenstein, Fischhoff & Phillips, 1982)
- **Tetlock's expert predictions study (2005)** — tracked 28,000+ predictions by 284 experts over 20 years. Most confident experts ("hedgehogs") performed worst. Tentative thinkers ("foxes") did better
- **Dunning-Kruger (1999)** — the least competent are most likely to overestimate their ability. The double curse: lack of skill prevents you from recognizing lack of skill
- **Confirmation bias (Lord, Ross & Lepper, 1979)** — presenting mixed evidence on a controversial topic made both sides MORE certain. The opposite of rational updating
- **Belief perseverance** — even when the basis for a belief is completely debunked, people continue holding it
- **Kahneman's WYSIATI** — "What You See Is All There Is." We build confident narratives from incomplete information
- **Einstellung effect** — expert chess players miss novel solutions because pattern recognition locks onto familiar approaches
- **Julia Galef's "Scout Mindset" (2021)** — contrasts soldier mindset (defend beliefs) with scout mindset (find truth). The desire to be right in the future should outweigh the desire to have been right in the past
- **Adam Grant's "confident humility"** — confident in your ability to learn, humble about current knowledge

### Real-world examples

- **Kodak** — engineer Steve Sasson invented the digital camera in 1975. Leadership was certain people would always want prints. Invested billions in digital but certain the transition would be slow. Bankrupt 2012
- **Blockbuster** — CEO Jim Keyes, 2008: "Neither RedBox nor Netflix are even on the radar screen." Had the chance to buy Netflix for $50M in 2000. Bankrupt by 2010
- **Nokia** — 40%+ global phone market share in 2007. Certain hardware quality and carrier relationships were what mattered. Acquired by Microsoft 2013
- **Challenger (1986)** — engineers warned about O-rings in cold. NASA management was certain the shuttle was safe. Feynman later showed NASA estimated failure at 1 in 100,000; actual rate was closer to 1 in 100
- **Stomach ulcers** — medical establishment was certain they were caused by stress. Marshall & Warren proposed H. pylori bacteria in 1982, were ridiculed. Marshall drank a petri dish to prove it. Nobel Prize 2005. Certainty delayed a cure by years
- **Continental drift** — Wegener proposed it in 1912. Geologists were certain continents were fixed. Took 50 years to accept plate tectonics
- **Semmelweis and handwashing** — proposed doctors wash hands between autopsies and deliveries, 1840s. Mortality dropped. Dismissed because "a gentleman's hands are clean." Died in an asylum

### Counterarguments

- **Analysis paralysis** — too much doubt is paralyzing. Bezos's 70% rule: decide when you have 70% of the info you wish you had
- **Startups require conviction** — every successful founder was told they were wrong. Perfect intellectual humility might mean never building anything
- **Certainty is functionally required** in surgery, emergency response, crisis leadership
- **The humility trap** — there's a difference between epistemic humility (holding beliefs tentatively) and performative humility (refusing to commit)
- **Confident humility** — you can have high conviction AND acknowledge you might be wrong. Bezos's "disagree and commit"

## Suggested Angle

The opening writes itself: everyone's certain Twain said this quote. He didn't. That's the piece in miniature.

From there, the conversation explores where this shows up — in tech, in business, in how we think about AI right now. We're in a moment of maximum certainty about a technology we understand least. "AI will replace everyone" and "AI is just autocomplete" are both said with equal conviction.

But the personal angle matters too — the certainties we carry about ourselves. "I'm not technical." "I'm too old for this." "I know what I'm good at." Those quiet certainties become invisible ceilings.

The counterargument is real: you need conviction to build anything. The distinction isn't certainty vs. doubt — it's whether you can articulate what would change your mind. If you can't, you're in dangerous territory.
