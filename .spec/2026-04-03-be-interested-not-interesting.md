## Research

### Key findings

- **Dale Carnegie popularized the idea** in *How to Win Friends and Influence People* (1936): "You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than you can in two years by trying to get other people interested in you." The exact phrase "be interested, not interesting" is a distillation that emerged over time
- **Harvard study (Huang et al., 2017)** — people who ask more follow-up questions are perceived as significantly more likable. In speed-dating experiments, more questions = more second dates. Follow-up questions specifically signal active listening
- **Harvard neuroscience (Tamir & Mitchell, 2012)** — talking about yourself activates the brain's reward centers (same regions as food and money). When you ask questions and let someone talk, you're giving them a neurological reward they associate with you
- **Todd Kashdan's curiosity research** — curious people were rated by strangers as more attractive and interesting after brief conversations. Being interested paradoxically makes you more interesting
- **Adam Grant's "Give and Take"** — givers (who show genuine interest in others) had 50% higher revenue than takers in a pharmaceutical sales study
- **Google's Project Oxygen** — top manager behaviors include being a good coach and empowering teams, both requiring deep curiosity about team members
- **Zenger & Folkman (2016)** — best listeners act like trampolines, asking questions that help people jump to new insights rather than just absorbing
- **Satya Nadella shifted Microsoft** from "know-it-all" to "learn-it-all" culture when he became CEO. Market cap grew from ~$300B to $3T+ during his tenure
- **Terry Gross** has described her job as being "a professional interested person"

### Counterarguments

- **Competence first**: The Pratfall Effect (Aronson, 1966) shows vulnerability only helps if you've first established competence. Pure interest without demonstrated value can read as having nothing to offer
- **Job interviews and pitches**: In competitive contexts, you MUST signal your qualities — pure curiosity doesn't land the job
- **The doormat problem**: Always being interested without contributing your own perspective leads to being perceived as lacking substance. It can also enable narcissists
- **Reciprocity matters**: The "36 Questions to Fall in Love" study (Aron et al., 1997) shows mutual vulnerability builds the deepest connections, not one-sided curiosity
- **Cultural context**: In some cultures, demonstrating your organization's prestige is an expected part of relationship-building before deeper inquiry begins
- **Power dynamics**: "Be interested" reads differently depending on who holds power. For marginalized groups, it can feel like advice to stay small

## Suggested Angle

The hook is the **AI tension**. In 2026, "being interesting" — having facts, articulate responses, polished content — is a commodity. Anyone can generate impressive-sounding output with AI. What's scarce is genuine human curiosity. The ability to ask the right question is now worth more than having the right answer.

But there's a paradox worth exploring: the entire creator economy and LinkedIn culture rewards broadcasting (being interesting), while the research overwhelmingly shows that curiosity (being interested) builds stronger relationships and better outcomes. Most professionals know this instinctively but still default to "impressive mode" in meetings, networking events, and online.

Write this from personal experience as a builder/developer. Keep it practical — not a psychology lecture but a reflection on what actually works when you stop trying to impress people. The Carnegie reference is the anchor, the Harvard research is the proof, and the AI angle is what makes it timely.
